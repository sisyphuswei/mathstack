Introduction

Sometimes we just use \(C_i\) and \(C_{i+1}\) to emphasis order. The filtration has infinite version but in this note we focus on finite filtration. A finite filtration can be seen as a series of simplex addition, so we often represented as a list and feed it into algorithm.

%\begin{ex} % This filtration (insert a diagram) can be represented as python list [([a], 0), ([b], 0), ([c], 1), ([d, 1]), % ([a, b], 1), ([b, c], 1), ([c, d], 2), ([a, d], 2), ([a, c], 3), ([a, b, c], 4), ([a, c, d], 5)]
%\end{ex}

For each \(K_i\) one can compute its homology. The inclusion map from \(C_i\) to \(C_{i+1}\) induce homomorphism between homology groups. These homology groups and induced homomorphisms is one example of the so called persistent module.

According to [Computing Persistent Homology, Zomorodian, Carlsson], we can equip \(R(t)\) with a graded ring structure and equip persistence module with a graded module structure over \(R(t)\). Then by commutative algebra we obtain a classification theorem for finite type persistence module. This classification is indeed a decomposition of persistence module into tiny building blocks called interval modules (as in [Chazal et al.]) or intervals. The intuition behind interval is quite simple: as new simplices adding to a complex, new class may born for new cycle, and old class may die for new boundary. The life of a class is encode in its interval. For example, suppose we have two points \(a\) and \(b\), and a new simplex \([a, b]\) is added. The existing connected components \(a\) and \(b\) are merged together. We may say \(a\) or \(b\) is dead because of \([a, b]\). Or imagine two circles of a cylinder. The cylinder merges two circles together and reduce the number of classes from two to one.

We first fix some notations. The finite filtration is thought of as adding one simplex \(\sigma_i\) at step \(i\) (\(1 \leq i \leq n\)), which is done in time \(t_i\), and \(\{t_i\}\) is a non-decreasing sequence. Denote the module generated by \(\{\sigma_1,...,\sigma_i\}\) as \(C^i\), containing all subcomplexes of \(C^i\). For a element in \(C^i\), its leading term is said to be the \(\sigma\) with the largest index. For example \(\sigma_1 + \sigma_2 + \sigma_3 \in C^3\) has leading term \(\sigma_3\).
For simplicity, throughout this section we use \(\intg_2\) coefficient.

Rather than give a proof of the above theorem, we introduce here an algorithm that computes the new basis. The algorithm appears in [Edelsbruner, Harer] and [Zomorodian \(\&\) Carlsson] in a slightly differnt form. Our running example is [(a, 0), (b, 0), (c, 1), (d, 1), (ab, 1), (bc, 1), (cd, 2), (ad, 2), (ac, 3), (abc, 4), (acd, 5)]. We perform the algorithm in details and it will be clear that a new basis is produced.

The matrix of \(\partial_1\) is

\(\bordermatrix{ & ab,1 & bc,1 & cd,2 & ad,2 & ac,3 \cr d, 1 & 0 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 \cr c, 1 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 & 1 \cr b, 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr a, 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \cr}\).

Any row or column where bold 1 resides is called pivot row or pivot column. The simplex above a pivot colume is said to be pivot simplex. Now apply column gaussian elimination. The leftmost nonzero element in row \(d\) is in column \(cd\). Use column \(cd\) to eliminate elements on the right, and then move column \(cd\) to the leftmost, we get

\(\bordermatrix{ & cd,2 & ab,1 & bc,1 & ad+cd,2 & ac,3 \cr d, 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr c, 1 & 1 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 1 \cr b, 0 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 & 0 \cr a, 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 \cr}\).

Do the same for column \(bc\), we get

\(\bordermatrix{ & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & ad+cd+bc,2 & ac+bc,3 \cr d, 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr c, 1 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr b, 0 & 0 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 1 \cr a, 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \cr}\).

Do the same for \(ab\). Now the matrix of \(\partial_1\) becomes

\(\bordermatrix{ & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & z_1,2 & z_2,3 \cr d,1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr c,1 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr b,0 & 0 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 \cr a,0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \cr}\),

where \(z_1 = ad+cd+bc+ab\), and \(z_2 = ac+bc+ab\). \(z_1, z_2\) are going to be used for the computation of \(\partial_2\). This is a column echelon form. The bold elements are of importance and the rows they reside are called pivot rows. Apply the row elementary operation we get the normal form

\(\bordermatrix{ & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & z_1,2 & z_2,3 \cr d+c,1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr c+b,1 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr b+a,0 & 0 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 \cr a,0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \cr}\).

Now we deal with \(\partial_2\). At first glance we may think it is a little hard to write down \(\partial_2\) with respect to \(\{z_1, z_2\}\), but indeed it is quite easy. Generally \(z_i\) is obtained by addition of pivot columns so \(z_i\) is the sum of its leading term and a linear combination of pivot simplices. For this reason we only have to look at the coefficients of \(ac\) and \(ad\) in \(\partial abc\) and \(\partial acd\) to determine \(\partial_2\) with respect to \(\{z_1, z_2\}\), which is \(\bordermatrix{ & abc,4 & acd,5 \cr z_2, 3 & 1 & 1 \cr z_1, 2 & 0 & 1 \cr}\), Perform column operation, we get \(\bordermatrix{ & abc,4 & abc+acd,5 \cr z_2, 3 & 1 & 0 \cr z_1, 2 & 0 & 1 \cr}\).

Now our new basis is \(\{ a, b+a, c+b, d+c, ab, bc, cd, ad+cd+bc+ab, ac+bc+ab, abc, abc+acd \}\) and \(F = \{ 1 \}\) and the pairs of \(G\) and \(H\) are \((2,5), (3,6), (4,7), (8,11)\), and \((9, 10)\). The barcodes of this filtration is thus \(\{[1,2), [0,1), [3,4), [2,5), [0, \infty) \}\). Barcodes infered from \(\partial_1\) and \(\partial_2\) matrices are related to \(H_0\) and \(H_1\) respectively. You may note that other new basis exist, like \(\{ a, b+a, c+b, d+b, ab, bc, cd+bc, ad+cd+bc+ab, ac+bc+ab, abc, abc+acd \}\), but corresponds to the same barcodes, which is the information we care most about.

We can apply persistent homology to atoms of proteins to extract features. An straightforward attempt to do it can be found in [Xia & Wei, 2014]. A more sophiscated approach distinguish different chemical elements such as C, H, O etc. This approach extracts more features.
