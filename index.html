A Very Short Note on Persistent Homology

Posted on 2020-06-10

Introduction

A filtration \(C=\{C_r | r \in \mathbb R\}\) is a group of finite simplicial complex indexed over real numbers such that each \(K_a\) is a simplicial complex and \(C_a \subset C_b\) for \(a \leq b\). Let \(C_{-\infty}= \bigcap C_a\) and \(C_{\infty} = \bigcup C_a\).

Sometimes we just use \(C_i\) and \(C_{i+1}\) to emphasis order. The filtration has infinite version but in this note we focus on finite filtration. A finite filtration can be seen as a series of simplex addition, so we often represented as a list and feed it into algorithm.

For each \(K_i\) one can compute its homology. The inclusion map from \(C_i\) to \(C_{i+1}\) induce homomorphism between homology groups. These homology groups and induced homomorphisms is one example of the so called persistent module.

A persistence module \(M\) is a family of \(R-\)modules \(M_i\), together with homomorphisms \(f_i : M_i \rightarrow M_{i+1}\).

According to [ZC05]1, we can equip \(R(t)\) with a graded ring structure and equip persistence module with a graded module structure over \(R(t)\). Then by commutative algebra we obtain a classification theorem for finite type persistence module. This classification is indeed a decomposition of persistence module into tiny building blocks called interval modules or intervals. The intuition behind interval is quite simple: as new simplices adding to a complex, new class may born for new cycle, and old class may die for new boundary. The life of a class is encode in its interval. For example, suppose we have two points \(a\) and \(b\), and a new simplex \([a, b]\) is added. The existing connected components \(a\) and \(b\) are merged together. We may say \(a\) or \(b\) is dead because of \([a, b]\). Or imagine two circles of a cylinder. The cylinder merges two circles together and reduce the number of classes from two to one.

Algorithm

We first fix some notations. The finite filtration is thought of as adding one simplex \(\sigma_i\) at step \(i\) (\(1 \leq i \leq n\)), which is done in time \(t_i\), and \(\{t_i\}\) is a non-decreasing sequence. Denote the module generated by \(\{\sigma_1,...,\sigma_i\}\) as \(C^i\), containing all subcomplexes of \(C^i\). For a element in \(C^i\), its leading term is said to be the \(\sigma\) with the largest index. For example \(\sigma_1 + \sigma_2 + \sigma_3 \in C^3\) has leading term \(\sigma_3\). For simplicity, throughout this section we use \(\mathbb Z_2\) coefficient.

There exists a partition \(\{1,2,...,n\} = F\sqcup G\sqcup H\) with a bijective pairing between \(G\) and \(H\). Moreover there is a new basis \(\{\hat{\sigma}_1, \hat{\sigma}_2, ..., \hat{\sigma}_n\}\) of \(C^n\) such that:

1. \(C^i\) is spanned by \(\hat{\sigma}_1, \hat{\sigma}_2, ..., \hat{\sigma}_i\) for all \(i\). Note that this is equivalent to the assertion that the leading term of each \(\hat{\sigma}_i\) is \(\sigma_i\) up to a nonzero scalar.

2. Any \(\hat{\sigma}_i\) has a well-defined dimension.

3. \(\partial \hat{\sigma}_f = 0\) for all \(f \in F\).

4. \(\partial \hat{\sigma}_h = \hat{\sigma}_g\) (and \(\partial \hat{\sigma}_g = 0\)), for all pairs of \(G\) and \(H\). This implies that \(h > g\), for \(\hat{\sigma}_g\), the boundary of \(\hat{\sigma}_h\), is a subcomplex of \(\hat{\sigma}_h\).

Once we obtain the new basis, we are able to compute the homology of \(C^i\) for all \(i\). \(Z_k\) contains the submodule generated by all \(k\)-dimensional \(\hat{\sigma}_f\)'s and \(\hat{\sigma}_g\)'s (\(f,g \le i\)). Moreover the boundary of any nontrivial linear combination of \(\hat{\sigma}_h\)'s (\(h \le i\)) is not zero since any nontrivial linear combination of \(\hat{\sigma}_g\)'s is nonzero. So \(Z_k\) is equal to the submodule generated by all \(k\)-dimensional \(\hat{\sigma}_f\)'s and \(\hat{\sigma}_g\)'s. \(B_k\), the image of boundary operator, is equal to the module generated by \(k\)-dimensional \(\hat{\sigma}_h\)'s. Because of the pairing of \(G\) and \(H\), we get a explicit description of \(H_k\) with respect to the new basis.

Any pair \(\{g, h\}\) implies that a homology class \(\hat{\sigma}_g\) is born at \(t_g\) and died at \(t_h\) because of \(\hat{\sigma}_h\). \([t_g,t_h)\) is said to be a barcode. As to any \(\hat{\sigma}_f\), it is always a homology class since \(t_f\), corresponding to barcode \([t_f, \infty)\).

Rather than give a proof of the above theorem, we introduce here an algorithm that computes the new basis. The algorithm appears in [EH02]2 and [ZC05]3 but takes a slightly different form. Our running example is [(a, 0), (b, 0), (c, 1), (d, 1), (ab, 1), (bc, 1), (cd, 2), (ad, 2), (ac, 3), (abc, 4), (acd, 5)].

The matrix of \(\partial_1\) is

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} & ab,1 & bc,1 & cd,2 & ad,2 & ac,3 \\ \hline d, 1 & 0 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 \\ c, 1 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 & 1 \\ b, 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ a, 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\]

Any row or column where bold 1 resides is called a pivot row or pivot column. The simplex above a pivot column is said to be pivot simplex. Now apply column gaussian elimination. The leftmost nonzero element in row \(d\) is in column \(cd\). Use column \(cd\) to eliminate elements on the right, and then move column \(cd\) to the leftmost, we get

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} & cd,2 & ab,1 & bc,1 & ad+cd,2 & ac,3 \\ \hline d, 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ c, 1 & 1 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 1 \\ b, 0 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ a, 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\]

Do the same for column \(bc\), we get

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & ad+cd+bc,2 & ac+bc,3 \\ \hline d, 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ c, 1 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ b, 0 & 0 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 1 & 1 \\ a, 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\]

Do the same for \(ab\). Now the matrix of \(\partial_1\) becomes

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & z_1,2 & z_2,3 \\ \hline d,1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ c,1 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ b,0 & 0 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 \\ a,0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\]

where \(z_1 = ad+cd+bc+ab\), and \(z_2 = ac+bc+ab\). \(z_1, z_2\) are going to be used for the computation of \(\partial_2\). This is a column echelon form. The bold elements are of importance and the rows they reside are called pivot rows. Apply the row elementary operation we get the normal form

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & z_1,2 & z_2,3 \\ \hline d+c,1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ c+b,1 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ b+a,0 & 0 & 0 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 \\ a,0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\]

Now we deal with \(\partial_2\). At first glance we may think it is a little hard to write down \(\partial_2\) with respect to \(\{z_1, z_2\}\), but indeed it is quite easy. Generally \(z_i\) is obtained by addition of pivot columns so \(z_i\) is the sum of its leading term and a linear combination of pivot simplices. For this reason we only have to look at the coefficients of \(ac\) and \(ad\) in \(\partial abc\) and \(\partial acd\) to determine \(\partial_2\) with respect to \(\{z_1, z_2\}\), which is

\[\begin{array}{c|cc} & abc,4 & acd,5 \\ \hline z_2, 3 & \textbf{1} & 1 \\ z_1, 2 & 0 & \textbf{1} \end{array}\]

Perform column operation, we get

\[\begin{array}{c|cc} & abc,4 & abc+acd,5 \\ \hline z_2, 3 & \textbf{1} & 0 \\ z_1, 2 & 0 & \textbf{1} \end{array}\]

Now our new basis is \[\{ a, b+a, c+b, d+c, ab, bc, cd, ad+cd+bc+ab, ac+bc+ab, abc, abc+acd \}.\] \(F = \{ 1 \}\) and the pairs of \(G\) and \(H\) are \((2,5), (3,6), (4,7), (8,11)\), and \((9, 10)\). The barcodes of this filtration is thus \(\{[1,2), [0,1), [3,4), [2,5), [0, \infty) \}\) (we ignore \([1, 1)\)). Barcodes infered from \(\partial_1\) and \(\partial_2\) matrices are related to \(H_0\) and \(H_1\) respectively. You may note that other basis exist, like \[\{ a, b+a, c+b, d+b, ab, bc, cd+bc, ad+cd+bc+ab, ac+bc+ab, abc, abc+acd \}\] but corresponds to the same barcodes, and that is the information we really care about.

A few remarks:

(1) Keep in mind that when perform column operation, only do it from left to right, otherwise condition 1 may not be satisfied.

(2) As we care most about computing barcodes, we may modify the above algorithm a little bit. We don't really have to keep track of complexes. For example, we may just reduce \(\partial_1\) to the following form

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc} & cd,2 & bc,1 & ab,1 & ad,2 & ac,3 \\ \hline d,1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ c,1 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ b,0 & 0 & 1 & \textbf{1} & 0 & 0 \\ a,0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\]

and state that a complex born because of \(d\) is destroyed by the addition of \(cd\), and the corresponding barcode is \([1,2)\).
